You have a plot, an existing building, or an idea. Before you commission an architect to plan it, there's a phase that determines everything that follows. It's called Phase Zero — and most clients don't know it exists.

## The most expensive mistakes happen before Service Phase 1

A project rarely fails at the execution-planning stage. It fails because of wrong assumptions made much earlier: a plot that doesn't actually allow what was hoped for under planning law. A use that doesn't work economically. Stakeholders who were brought in too late.

Phase Zero is the moment when these assumptions are put to the test — in a structured, methodical way, with a clear outcome.

## What you take away from a Phase Zero as a client

A well-executed project preparation gives you concrete answers to the most important questions:

**Is my project feasible?** Planning law, existing structure, access — what is actually possible?

**Which use makes sense?** Not every intended use fits every site. Phase Zero surfaces alternatives and evaluates them objectively.

**What will it really cost?** No guarantee, but a reliable order of magnitude — based on real parameters, not wishful thinking.

**What do I need to decide, and when?** A clear recommendation: keep planning, adjust course, or rethink entirely.

## When is a feasibility study especially worthwhile?

Phase Zero services aren't equally relevant to every project. They're particularly valuable for:

- **Change-of-use and conversion projects** — when an existing building is to take on a new function
- **Existing properties with unclear potential** — when it's unclear what's possible under planning law and economically
- **Purchase decisions** — before a plot or building is bought
- **Projects with multiple use scenarios** — when different options need to be weighed against each other
